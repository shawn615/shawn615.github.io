3D Generation 1. PAD: Pose-Aware Diffusion for 3D Generation

Title
Pose-Aware Diffusion for 3D Generation
Authors
Zihan Zhou*, Luxi Chen*, Jingzhi Zhou, Yuhao Wan, Min Zhao, Baoyu Fan, Chongxuan Li
Affiliation
Renmin University of China
Links
arXiv · Code

Summary

Most image-to-3D methods (TRELLIS, Hunyuan3D, TripoSG) generate in a canonical coordinate space — always facing a fixed orientation — then estimate the input image's pose and rotate. This canonical-then-rotate paradigm is fragile: symmetric objects produce degenerate pose estimates, and fine-grained details from the input are lost during the rotation step.

PAD eliminates this entirely by generating directly in observation space. A monocular depth estimator (MoGe) produces a partial point cloud, which is encoded through a vecset VAE and concatenated with the noisy diffusion latent along the sequence dimension. The DiT-based flow matching model then "grows" the complete mesh outward from this 3D anchor.

PAD generates directly in observation space via point cloud conditioning, eliminating the need for post-hoc pose estimation. (Figure from the paper)

PAD pipeline: monocular depth → unproject → VAE encode → concat with diffusion latent → DiT → posed mesh.

Compositional Scene Generation

Because each object is generated in camera coordinates, multi-object scenes assemble via simple union: $M_{\text{scene}} = \bigcup_i M^{(i)}$. No per-object pose optimization or registration needed.

Compositional scene assembly: each object generated independently, combined by union in camera coordinates. (Figure from the paper)

Why It Matters

  • Point cloud > depth map for conditioning: lifting to 3D before encoding preserves spatial structure that cross-attention depth conditioning loses.
  • Depth noise augmentation (FOV perturbation, estimator swap, off-center translation) prevents train-test gap collapse.
  • Compositional scenes for free — a natural consequence of observation-space generation, immediately useful for AR/VR asset placement.

Ablation: (a) no 3D condition → structural distortion, (b) depth map instead of PC → detail loss, (c) no noise augmentation → surface collapse, (d) full model. (Figure from the paper)

3DGS 2. CrossGrad-GS: Two-View Accumulation for Hybrid-Capture Splatting

Title
Two-View Accumulation as the Primary Training Lever for Hybrid-Capture Gaussian Splatting: A Variance-Decomposition View of When Gradient Surgery Helps
Authors
Sungjun Cho
Affiliation
HKUST
Links
arXiv

Summary

Hybrid-capture scenes (aerial drones + ground-level cameras) suffer 1–3 dB under-fitting in the minority capture regime. This paper exhaustively tests sophisticated solutions — gradient surgery, magnitude correction, active pairing, projective preconditioning — and finds a stunning negative result: none of them outperform simply rendering two random views per optimizer step.

Hybrid-capture scenes demand one 3DGS representation for both near (high-frequency detail) and far (global consistency) views. (Figure from the paper)

Variance-Decomposition Framework

The theoretical explanation is elegant. For a two-view gradient estimator:

$$\frac{\text{Var}(\hat{g}_R)}{\text{Var}(\hat{g}_S)} = 1 + \frac{\sigma_b^2}{\sigma_w^2}$$

Structured pairing only helps when $\sigma_b^2 / \sigma_w^2$ is appreciably positive. Empirically, $\sigma_b^2 \ll \sigma_w^2$ on all 5 benchmarks — individual Gaussians' gradients are dominated by per-view visibility noise, not by the near/far regime label.

Exhaustive evaluation: only the structural change (two views per step) moves PSNR beyond seed variance.

Practical takeaway: For hybrid aerial-ground 3DGS training, just render two random views per step. Don't bother with gradient surgery. The contribution is the honest characterization, not the method.

Editing 3. GOR-IS: Object Removal in the Intrinsic Space

Title
GOR-IS: 3D Gaussian Object Removal in the Intrinsic Space
Authors
Yonghao Zhao, Yupeng Gao, Jian Yang, Jin Xie, Beibei Wang
Affiliation
Nankai University, Nanjing University of Science and Technology
Links
arXiv · Project

Summary

Object removal in 3DGS looks solved until you try it on a reflective scene. Remove a red ball from a glossy floor → the red caustic remains. Prior methods (SPInNeRF, GScream) operate in RGB space with no light transport model. GOR-IS fixes this by decomposing 3DGS into intrinsic components (albedo $a$, roughness $r$, metalness $m$) + environment map, then inpainting in material space instead of RGB.

The core challenge: prior methods leave ghost reflections. GOR-IS suppresses them via intrinsic-space reasoning. (Figure from the paper)

GOR-IS framework: intrinsic reconstruction → lighting-aware masking → material-space inpainting → re-lighting. (Figure from the paper)

Three-Stage Pipeline

GOR-IS operates on materials instead of RGB: lighting-aware masking catches reflection zones, intrinsic inpainting fills them physically.

Why It Matters

  • Object removal is a light transport problem, not just texture filling. Intrinsic decomposition is the principled fix.

  • Screen-space mipmap filtering for glossy reflections replaces expensive Monte Carlo at a fraction of the cost.

Pose 4. Adaptive Geodesic Conformal Prediction for Egocentric Pose

Title
Adaptive Geodesic Conformal Prediction for Egocentric Camera Pose Estimation
Authors
Aishani Pathak, Hasti Seifi
Affiliation
Arizona State University
Links
arXiv

Summary

Conformal prediction (CP) gives distribution-free coverage guarantees for any predictor. But standard CP with a single global threshold achieves 90% overall coverage while collapsing to ~60% on the hardest 25% of frames (Q4) — rapid head motion, occlusions, blur. The ~30 percentage point conditional gap means uncertainty estimates are unreliable when you need them most.

This paper introduces two fixes: (1) geodesic SE(3) nonconformity scoring that respects the Riemannian structure of the pose manifold, and (2) DINOv2-Bridge adaptive thresholding that uses frozen DINOv2 features + a small Bridge MLP to estimate per-frame difficulty without ground-truth poses at test time.

Two innovations: geodesic SE(3) scoring + DINOv2-Bridge adaptive thresholding for per-frame calibration.

Why It Matters

  • Metric geometry matters for CP: geodesic vs. Euclidean identifies fundamentally different hard cases (only 15-26% Q4 overlap). For any non-Euclidean space (rotations, distributions), the scoring function changes what CP protects against.
  • DINOv2 as universal difficulty oracle: frozen features + Bridge MLP predicts difficulty at test time without ground-truth — a pattern that generalizes beyond pose.
  • No retraining of the base predictor needed — wraps any black-box with calibration-based uncertainty sets.

ICML 3DGS 5. LeGS: Learnable Density Control for 3D Gaussian Splatting

Title
Beyond Heuristics: Learnable Density Control for 3D Gaussian Splatting (ICML 2026)
Authors
Zhenhua Ning, Xin Li, Jun Yu, Guangming Lu, Yaowei Wang, Wenjie Pei
Affiliation
HIT Shenzhen, Peng Cheng Laboratory
Links
arXiv · Code

Summary

Vanilla 3DGS's density control (split if gradient > $\tau_g$, clone if scale < $\tau_s$, prune if opacity < $\alpha_{\min}$) is the last hand-crafted component. These thresholds are scene-dependent, have no global context, and conflate gradient magnitude with reconstruction need. LeGS replaces this with a PPO-trained policy network that outputs per-Gaussian actions {maintain, clone, split, prune}.

LeGS: policy network takes per-Gaussian features → outputs action probabilities. Sensitivity-based reward measures individual contribution. PPO with maintain-action baseline for stable training. (Figure from the paper)

O(N) Closed-Form Sensitivity Score

The key enabling idea: instead of brute-force leave-one-out rendering ($O(N \times H \times W)$ per Gaussian), derive the counterfactual color analytically from alpha compositing:

$$C_{-i}(\mathbf{p}) = \frac{C(\mathbf{p}) - \alpha_i T_i c_i}{1 - \alpha_i T_i}$$

This gives each Gaussian's sensitivity score $\text{Sen}_i = \sum_{\mathbf{p} \in \mathcal{V}_i} \|C(\mathbf{p}) - C_{-i}(\mathbf{p})\|_2$ in a single forward pass — making RL training tractable over millions of primitives.

LeGS: per-Gaussian features → policy MLP → {maintain, clone, split, prune}. Sensitivity reward via closed-form leave-one-out.

Why It Matters

  • RL for representation management is viable — the closed-form sensitivity score makes it tractable. This framework could extend to voxel grids, hash tables, or mesh refinement.
  • Pareto-dominant: better quality and fewer Gaussians simultaneously, rare in 3DGS literature.
  • No manual threshold tuning — the policy adapts per-scene, per-iteration. Complex foreground gets densified; simple background gets pruned.

Today's Themes

Three threads stand out across today's picks:

  1. "Where" matters as much as "how": PAD shows that generating in observation space beats canonical-then-rotate. GOR-IS shows that editing in intrinsic space beats RGB space. The coordinate system / representation space you choose changes which problems remain.
  2. Simple baselines expose complex methods: CrossGrad-GS demonstrates that two random views per step matches every gradient surgery variant tested. The contribution is the honest characterization — and more papers should report informative negatives this way.
  3. Learning replaces heuristics: LeGS (RL for density control) and Geodesic CP (DINOv2-Bridge for adaptive thresholding) both replace hand-tuned components with learned alternatives — and both provide theoretical justification for why learning wins.
